# Student Research Topic Guide (UMBL Edition)

This guide is intended for students seeking topics for undergraduate theses, course papers, or independent research projects.  
UMBL (Universal Meaning‑Based Language) is an early‑stage **language‑design development project**, exploring how to construct a structured, evolvable pictographic writing language based on modular semantic components.  
UMBL is not yet a mature language; it is a **language engineering prototype** suitable for small‑scale, controlled, and verifiable academic studies.

This guide proposes possible research directions without claiming any definitive theoretical advantages for UMBL.

---

## 1. Introduction: Positioning UMBL as a Research Object

The core goal of UMBL is to explore a **pictographic writing language prototype**, including its character‑formation principles, semantic structures, potential grammar, and mechanisms for language evolution.  
UMBL is **not** an icon set or UI design project. It is a **language‑engineering experiment** that aims, in the long term, to develop into a fully functional constructed language—similar in ambition to Esperanto, but pictographic and evolvable.

The design motivation for UMBL arises from long‑term comparative observations of Chinese and English, including:

- Chinese character formation, semantic radicals, and visual transparency  
- English reliance on roots and affixes for abstract concept formation  
- The strong visual‑semantic tendencies in Chinese media culture (subtitles, danmu overlays)  
- Limitations of phonetic writing systems in cross‑cultural comprehension  
- Inconsistencies in natural languages regarding abstraction and logical transparency  

These observations motivate the question:  
**Is it possible to design a structured, compositional, and evolvable pictographic language that offers new possibilities for semantic transparency and cross‑cultural understanding?**

Because UMBL is open‑source, structurally clear, and easy to access, it is highly suitable as a research object for undergraduate students.

## 2. Possible Research Directions (By Discipline)

### 2.1 Linguistics (Core Area)
- Analysis of UMBL’s character‑formation principles  
- Structural study of pictographic morphemes  
- Combination logic of pictographic words  
- Exploration of preliminary grammar prototypes  
- Structural comparison between UMBL and Chinese character components  
- Comparison with constructed language (conlang) design methodologies  
- Feasibility analysis of UMBL’s evolution rules  

### 2.2 Cognitive Science
- Learning curves for pictographic languages  
- Recognition speed and cognitive load of pictographic symbols  
- Misinterpretation patterns in visual semantic structures  
- Processing differences between pictographic and linear textual expressions  

### 2.3 Information Design & Standardization
- Analysis of UMBL’s modular component design  
- Structural comparison with ISO graphical symbols  
- Evaluation of visual consistency and readability  
- Maintainability and extensibility of character‑formation rules  

### 2.4 Computer Science
- Encoding schemes and data structures for UMBL  
- Automated composition of pictographic expressions  
- Machine‑readable pictographic language structures  
- Prototype editors or rendering tools for UMBL  

### 2.5 Design Studies
- Experimental design of pictographic words based on UMBL  
- Methods for ensuring visual‑semantic consistency  
- Designer experience studies on constructing pictographic expressions  

---

## 3. Feasible Research Methods (Undergraduate‑Friendly)

### 3.1 Literature Review
- Constructed language (conlang) research  
- Pictographic writing and graphical symbol studies  
- Cognitive load and visual processing literature  
- Information visualization and standardization research  

### 3.2 Small‑Scale User Studies
- Recognition speed  
- Comprehension accuracy  
- Misinterpretation rates  
- Learning curves  
- Comparative experiments: pictographic morphemes vs. linear text  

### 3.3 Comparative Analysis
- Structural comparison with Chinese character components  
- Semantic transparency comparison with emoji or ISO pictograms  
- Structural comparison with other constructed languages (e.g., Esperanto, Lojban)  

### 3.4 Prototype Construction
- Building a small set of pictographic words  
- Designing simple pictographic sentence‑expression tasks  
- Testing semantic transparency and comprehensibility  

### 3.5 Corpus‑Style Quantitative Analysis
- Statistical analysis of UMBL component structures  
- Frequency and distribution of component combinations  
- Semantic domain coverage analysis  

---

## 4. Citable Materials 

- UMBL GitHub repository (component library, whitepaper, design guidelines)  
- Literature on constructed languages and language engineering  
- Research on pictographic writing and graphical symbols  
- Cognitive science and visual language studies  
- Public summaries of ISO graphical symbol standards  

Students should distinguish between **engineering project materials** and **peer‑reviewed academic literature**.

---

## 5. Important Notes

- UMBL is not a mature language; it is a language‑design prototype.  
- Grammar, phonology, and character‑formation rules are still exploratory.  
- Research should focus on **analysis**, **comparison**, and **exploration**, not on “proving UMBL’s superiority.”  
- Research scope should be small and well‑defined.  
- Limitations must be clearly acknowledged.  
- User studies must follow basic ethical guidelines.  

---

This guide aims to provide a practical starting point for student research.  
The value of UMBL lies not in offering definitive conclusions, but in serving as a **research‑ready, analyzable, and discussable language‑design prototype** that opens rich possibilities across linguistics, cognitive science, information design, and computer science.
